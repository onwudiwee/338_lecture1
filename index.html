The Overview

Dublin surprised me in every way I didn't expect to be surprised. I'd imagined rain-slicked streets and ruddy pub interiors — and I got those, of course — but I hadn't counted on the literary ghosts around every corner, the warmth of strangers in queues, or how the River Liffey looks at dusk when the Georgian lamps catch the water.

Three weeks. Three wildly different rhythms. This is how it happened, in order.

Favourite moment — Standing in Temple Bar at midnight, listening to trad music spill out onto the cobblestones.

Subpage · Week One

Arrivals & Temple Bar

Days 1–7 · Getting my bearings

I landed at Dublin Airport on a Tuesday morning with a rolling suitcase, jet lag, and absolutely no plan for lunch. The taxi driver immediately began telling me about the Dubs' chances in the championship. I nodded along. This felt like the right start.

Temple Bar became my gravitational centre that first week. Technically it's a neighbourhood, not just a pub — though the pub is certainly the beating heart of it. Cobblestones designed to slow you down, strings of fairy lights that make everything look like a film set, and live trad sessions that begin at 3pm and end whenever the musicians feel like it.

I ate fish and chips standing at a high table. I bought a second-hand paperback of Dubliners from a stall off Dame Street. I got completely lost trying to find Grogan's, and then walked past it three times. Week one felt like learning a new city's grammar.

Subpage · Week Two

Deeper into the City

Days 8–14 · Finding the locals' Dublin

By week two I'd stopped carrying my map. I knew which coffee shops opened early, which bus route skirted the quays most efficiently, and where to find proper brown bread. This felt like a victory.

I spent a long afternoon at the Chester Beatty Library — free, extraordinary, and almost always quieter than it deserves to be. Then a rainy evening at the National Gallery, where I sat in front of a Caravaggio for twenty minutes and didn't think about anything in particular.

The Georgian squares opened up to me this week. Merrion Square, Fitzwilliam Square — all that dignified symmetry and those famous coloured doors. I began photographing every door I passed. By Thursday I had 47 photographs of doors. I regret nothing.

Subpage · Week Three

Slow Days & Leaving

Days 15–21 · Making peace with departure

The last week always has a different quality. You start noticing the things you'll miss — the specific sound of seagulls over the Ha'penny Bridge, the way people say "grand" to mean approximately seventeen different things depending on inflection.

I took a day trip to Howth, a fishing village a short DART ride up the coast. Walked the cliff path. Ate smoked salmon from a paper bag. Watched seals in the harbour. It was the kind of afternoon that makes you recalibrate what a good day actually means.

My last evening back in Temple Bar. Same cobblestones, same music bleeding out of the same doors. But this time I wasn't lost. I ordered a Guinness, found a stool, and just listened. That felt like enough.

About Me

The Traveller

Bio

A wanderer with a notebook and a mild obsession with bookshops. I travel slowly, eat locally, and always stay longer than planned. Dublin was my third solo trip — and the first city that felt like it was actually glad I'd come.

Hobbies

Reading in cafés. Long walks with no fixed destination. Trad music (listening, not playing — I have tried). Street photography. Collecting postcards I never send. Finding the best brown bread in any given city.

Inspiration Behind This Site

I wanted a record that was more than an Instagram grid — something with texture and time. Travel writing taught me that the best souvenirs are the details you almost forgot: the smell of a bookshop, the sound of rain on cobblestones, the exact quality of light on the Liffey at dusk. This site is my attempt to keep those things alive.
